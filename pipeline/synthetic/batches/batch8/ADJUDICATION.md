A pearl is nacre laid down in patient layers around a seeded nucleus, and every disturbance — a panel hauled up to be scrubbed, a net turned over, a diver's shadow crossing the racks — is a jolt of stress, and stress, the old hands say, is a blemish waiting to happen. It is a calm, humane story, and it has shaped the way most farmers work. That gap was put to the test in a working bay. At the Alenga Bay pearl cooperative, a team led by the shellfish physiologist Renata Vasco followed six seasons of harvests across fourteen leases, holding the water temperature and the oyster strain steady while varying just one thing: whether a lease turned and cleaned its panels on a frequent cycle, unsettling the oysters again and again, or left them very largely alone. Measured by the sheer thickness of the coat, calm was simply more pearl.

Then Vasco's team stopped weighing the nacre and began grading what a buyer actually pays for. A pearl's worth follows the evenness of its layers far more than their bulk: a thick coat laid on crookedly is a lumpy, baroque thing worth little, while a thinner coat laid down true is round, lustrous and dear.
