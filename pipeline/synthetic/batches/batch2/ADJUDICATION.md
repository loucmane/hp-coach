### Text

Along the driest coasts on earth, where rain may not fall for years at a stretch, the air still carries water. Sea fog rolls in on most mornings and hangs against the hills until the sun burns it off, and for a few decades now engineers have tried to comb that moisture out of the sky with walls of fine mesh. String the screens across a ridge, the pitch goes, and droplets bead on the fibers, run down into a gutter, and fill a tank below — free water, wrung from a passing cloud. The idea is old, cheap, and almost impossibly appealing, and every few years it returns to the headlines dressed as a cure for the deserts that keep spreading. A recent trial on a fog-belt ridge above the fishing town of Corvane set out to test a narrower claim: that the same screens could keep newly planted trees alive.

The work was led by Dr. Ines Marlow of the Halvard Institute for Arid-Lands Research, whose team put roughly three thousand native thorn-tree seedlings into the ground over three planting seasons, from 2021 through 2023. Some were set behind panels of the mesh; others, the controls, were left in the open. The panels were nothing exotic — double-layered polypropylene netting of the sort used to shade greenhouses, strung between posts a couple of arms wide. On the seaward slope — the flank that meets the morning fog head-on — the difference was hard to miss. After two dry seasons, most of the netted seedlings there were still alive, on the order of two in three, while barely a fifth of the unnetted plants had survived. Marlow's hydrologists reckoned that each panel was delivering a few liters of water a day through the foggiest months, enough, it seemed, to carry a young root across the rainless stretch.

Then the tidy result began to complicate itself. On the leeward slope, tucked behind the crest where the fog thins to a haze, the panels bought almost nothing: the netted and unnetted seedlings died at much the same rate, and the two survival curves ran so close together that Marlow declined to call the gap real. What did keep seedlings alive over there was cruder. A small group on that same dry ground had been watered by hand, a few liters a week hauled in by truck, and roughly seven in ten of them pulled through — better than anything the nets had managed on the fog-poor slope. The water truck broke an axle twice on the switchbacks that first summer, which tells you something about how grudging the ground is. Whatever the screens were doing where they worked, it did not look like a property of the mesh itself. It looked like water, arriving often enough to matter.

Not everyone reads the numbers so cautiously. Rem Kessler, the engineer whose firm designs the mesh arrays, sees a technology on the verge of proving itself. 'People have spent a hundred years telling us these coasts are finished,' he told me, standing beside a panel silvered with beads of dew. 'We can green any dry shore the fog reaches, and the fog reaches a great deal more of the world than anyone has bothered to measure,' he added. It is a stirring pitch, and Kessler may yet be proved partly right; the seaward plots are, after all, real, and they are still standing.

For now, though, the Corvane trial points somewhere more modest than the pitch. The nets earn their keep where the fog is already thick and dependable, and they seem to do it not through any cleverness in the weave but by handing a seedling the one thing the coast withholds — a steady sip of water. Where the fog runs thin, the screens have little to catch, and no width of mesh can conjure moisture from a dry sky. That is a far smaller promise than a cure for the deserts. It may also be the only honest one.
