## las-b1-001 · LÄS — **MINOR_EDITS**

*Register:* Textbook populärvetenskaplig sakprosa and a strong corpus match: result-first research lede, named researcher plus a dissenting entomologist, closing byline (Torsten Bjällerup) with a three-term glossary tail, nominal/subordinated agent-light register, LIX in the 44–58 journalism band, and hedged scoped claims engineered for the distractor traps — the two defects are localized lexical/grammatical slips, not a register break.

**Findings & suggested edits:**

- **[major] verb calque** — quote: «Medan biologer noggrant kartlagt hur bin och humlor betjänar grödorna på dagen»  
  → **fix:** Medan biologer noggrant kartlagt hur bin och humlor pollinerar grödorna på dagen  
  *why:* betjänar grödorna is a calque of English 'serve the crops'; the native verb for what bees do to crops is pollinerar (the passage itself later uses the idiomatic Honungsbina, som sköter mycket av dagpollineringen).

- **[major] passive infinitive** — quote: «så att varje mörklagd äng hade en fortsatt upplyst tvilling att jämföras med»  
  → **fix:** så att varje mörklagd äng hade en fortsatt upplyst tvilling att jämföra med  
  *why:* The 'N att V-inf' purpose frame takes the active infinitive in native Swedish (cf. en bok att läsa, not att läsas); the passive jämföras is a grammatical interference error.

*Answer-key language:* Every stem, option and key is grammatical and unambiguous as language: keys D (Q1), A (Q2), C (Q3), B (Q4) each read cleanly and the distractors are well-formed Swedish with correct BIFF placement (e.g. Q1-D 'medan ängar längre bort inte förändrades', Q4-B 'De påverkades sannolikt inte nämnvärt'). No language change proposed here touches which option is correct. Note Q2-A's 'det svaga horisontljus' is the accepted bare-noun literary variant, not an error, and is left as-is.

## elf-b1-002 · ELF — **MINOR_EDITS**

*Register:* Authentic educated-magazine society commentary, squarely in the corpus's Economist/New-Statesman/society-culture bucket (~20% of long-form ELF): dry attributed voice ("Her tone is dry rather than triumphant"), a load-bearing named-source quote (Priya Mehta), and the corpus's signature sentence rhythm — short verdict sentences ("The prediction looked safe." / "Mehta is blunt about it.") beside long subordinated ones. **Findings & suggested edits:**

- **[minor] collocation** — quote: «their ___(2)___ has quietly deepened»  
  → **fix:** Keep the key 'popularity'. The fixed-frame verb 'deepened' pairs naturally with intensity nouns (appeal / affection / hold / loyalty), whereas 'popularity' idiomatically grows, spreads, or rises. Because 'deepened' is exactly what eliminates the 'familiarity' and 'prosperity' distractors, do NOT swap it here — escalate a possible verb change (e.g. 'their popularity has quietly grown/spread') to the trap gate so the distractor set can be re-balanced if the verb moves.  
  *why:* 'Popularity ... deepened' is a slightly marked collocation to a native ear (growth verbs are the default), but the verb is load-bearing for the option-(2) eliminations, so smoothing it is trap-design business, not a unilateral language edit.

- **[minor] collocation** — quote: «can ___(4)___ its slim margins overnight»  
  → **fix:** Keep the key 'erode' (erode + margins is the intended collocation); adjust only the adverbial, which is aspectually incompatible: e.g. 'can erode its slim margins in a single bad season' or 'can quietly erode its slim margins'. Dropping/replacing 'overnight' does not touch the key or any distractor.  
  *why:* 'Erode' denotes gradual attrition while 'overnight' denotes suddenness — a mild aspectual clash; fixing the adverb preserves the 'erode margins' collocation and the whole trap set.

*Answer-key language:* All five gaps present POS-uniform, suffix-rhymed single-word option sets (-ty / -ity / adjective / verb / -able), each grammatical and unambiguous in its frame; every key — novelty, popularity, resistant, erode, durable — reads grammatically and unambiguously in situ (grammaticality only; key correctness is G-KEY's job). ## elf-b1-003 · ELF — **MINOR_EDITS**

*Register:* Authentic mid-Atlantic science journalism, squarely in the corpus's New Scientist / Scientific American bucket (§4): named ecologist Marisol Venn, a fictional popular-science monthly, a quoted researcher coinage ('eavesdropping on someone else's dinner bell'), and a punchy 4-word verdict sentence ('The timing is no accident.') set against 30+-word subordinated sentences — matching the measured high within-passage sentence-length variance (sd/mean ≈0.46). Declared AmE is held (positive marker 'toward'); no BrE drift. Formal-magazine, not clickbait or textbook-flat.

**Findings & suggested edits:**

- **[major] collocation** — quote: «drive up dense clouds of night-flying moths»  
  → **fix:** flush up dense clouds of night-flying moths  
  *why:* 'drive up' collocates with quantities/prices, not with flushing insects into flight; native usage for putting moths on the wing is 'flush up' / 'stir up' / 'put up' (this is the hunt-list's named ELF collocation defect).

- **[minor] collocation** — quote: «the owls simply harvest the insects the bats stir loose»  
  → **fix:** the owls simply harvest the insects the bats shake loose  
  *why:* 'stir loose' is non-standard; the fixed English collocation for dislodging something into flight is 'shake/knock loose', and it also avoids a third weak flush-verb after 'drive up' and option C's 'stirs up'.

- **[minor] option-parallelism** — quote: «The owls following the bats through the trees in order to locate the insects.»  
  → **fix:** The owls' own pursuit of the bats through the trees in order to locate the insects.  
  *why:* Option D is a gerund-headed clause while A/B/C are noun-phrase-headed ('drop'/'daylight'/'departure'); recasting to an NP restores the corpus norm of four grammatically parallel option statements without changing the answer or defusing the 'chasing the bats' trap.

*Answer-key language:* All four options are grammatical and unambiguous as language. Key C ('The evening departure of the local bats, which stirs up the moths the owls eat.') is a clean one-sentence paraphrase whose relative clause reads unambiguously and does not recycle a distinctive content-word run from the passage — good key language. A and B are well-formed NP answers. The only language wobble is D's gerund-headed structure vs the NP heads of A/B/C (minor finding 3, with a trap-preserving recast). No key/option contains agreement, tense, or reference errors; nothing about the language makes more than one option defensible.
