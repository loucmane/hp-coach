### Text

For years the pitch to any glassmaker has run the same way: the more recycled glass you tip into the furnace, the greener the bottle that comes out the other end. Crushed waste glass — cullet — melts at a lower temperature than the raw mixture of sand, soda ash, and limestone it stands in for, so each load of it that goes in ought to trim the fuel the furnace burns and the carbon it breathes out. Fill the batch with cullet, the argument runs, and you save energy and landfill in a single stroke. It is a clean story, and it has shaped collection targets across the industry. What the story mostly skips is the awkward question of what happens when the recycled glass arriving at the furnace door is not clean at all.

That question was finally put to a working furnace. At the Marholm container works, a team led by the glass chemist Ottilie Brandt ran a season of matched melting campaigns, holding everything steady but the cullet — its share of the batch, and where it came from. Some campaigns were fed cullet that had been sorted by color and picked clean of everything but glass; others were fed the mixed stream that arrives straight from municipal collection, caps and crockery and all. Meters on the fuel line logged what each campaign burned. On energy the result was just as advertised, and pleasingly straight: each added tenth of cullet cut the furnace's fuel use by roughly three percent, clean stream or dirty, right across the range they tested. On the fuel line alone, more cullet was simply better.

Then Brandt's team looked past the fuel line at the glass itself, and the two streams parted company. Municipal cullet carries a small, stubborn load of things that are not glass — a chip of ceramic, a shard of stoneware, a fleck of stone off the roadside — and these do not melt at the temperature glass does. They ride on through the furnace as "stones", hard little inclusions that seed cracks and force the flawed glass to be pulled and melted a second time. With clean, sorted cullet the reject rate barely stirred, even when cullet ran most of the batch. With the mixed municipal stream it edged up slowly at first and then, once cullet passed about sixty percent, climbed sharply: each further ton of dirty glass carried its fixed freight of stones, and remelting the rejects handed back much of the fuel the cullet had saved. Read only the fuel line, and the greenest furnace is the fullest one. Read the reject bin too, and it is not.

This is not how the case is usually made. Rasmus Feld, who runs the regional glass-collection program that feeds the works, makes it with no such hedging. "Fill every furnace to the top with cullet — always, on every batch," he told me. "There is no such thing as too much recycled glass. If the melt comes out flawed, you clean the stream; you do not cut the cullet." Brandt is more guarded. The stream can indeed be cleaned, she agrees, but only so far and never for free: the last chips of ceramic are the hardest and dearest to pick out, and no municipal system she knows of delivers glass clean enough to run at the very top of the range without the stones coming in behind it.

The season's record, read whole, is more divided than Feld's rule allows. It does not show that cullet is a false economy — the fuel savings are real, and on a clean, sorted stream they run all the way up. What it shows is that "more cullet" and "greener glass" hold together only while the glass going in stays clean; past that point, on the mixed stream most works actually receive, the stones win back what the furnace saved. The number that matters, in the end, is not how full the batch is. It is how clean. A furnace fed the best cullet can swallow almost any amount of it. A furnace fed the worst has a ceiling, and it sits lower than the collection targets like to admit.
— Cornelia Stahl

For three centuries the pilots who worked the Verran coast steered by a craft the chartmakers rather looked down on. They kept no fixed longitude and trusted no single instrument. Instead each pilot carried a worn logbook — a tally — in which, watch by watch, he set down the ship's heading, his own guess at her speed, and the hours she had run, and from these figures he reckoned by arithmetic alone how far the vessel had crept along an unseen line, east or west, since her last sure landmark. The method had a name the guild used without embarrassment: dead reckoning. It was, on the face of it, a chain of guesses. A misjudged current, an over-generous estimate of speed, a helmsman's drift while the pilot slept — any one of these could throw the whole reckoning off by a day's sail, and no chart aboard could catch the error.

The standard account of how this navigation grew safer is a story about instruments. On that telling, the coast was tamed as better tools arrived — the log-line to measure speed, the traverse board to record each change of heading, and at last the sea-going clock — each one replacing some part of the pilot's fallible senses with a reading he could not fudge. There is real evidence for the story. The charts engraved after 1680, when a workshop at Halden began printing plates instead of selling hand-copied sheets, are demonstrably more consistent with one another than anything the coast had known before, and wrecks on the worst headlands did grow rarer across the same decades.

However, when the historian Ottilie Brandt worked through the surviving tallies of the Verran guild — some six hundred of them, kept together in a single harbour archive — she found the instruments arriving later than the story needs them to. The reckoning grew dependable, she argues, a full generation before the good clocks did. What changed first was not the pilots' tools but their habit of pooling what each of them knew. From about 1650 the guild required every returning pilot to copy his run into a common book, so that a young man setting out could read how forty predecessors had judged the very same stretch of water in the very same season. Where one tally erred, the forty tended to average the error away.

Brandt is careful not to claim too much. "The shared book did not make any single voyage more accurate," she writes. "It made the guild's collective estimate less wrong, which is a slower and humbler thing." The distinction matters, because a pilot out at sea still had only his own reckoning to steer by on the night it counted. And the pooled record carried a bias of its own: voyages that ended in wreck seldom came back to be copied into the book, so the common record quietly favoured the routes and the seasons that had merely happened to be survivable. The east–west problem — fixing how far along the line a ship had truly run — was never really solved by the tallies. It was, at best, hedged: narrowed from a wild guess to an educated one.

So the tidy lesson — that finer instruments made the coast safe — survives only in part. The sea-clock, when it finally came, did what no logbook could, and Brandt does not pretend otherwise. But the record of the Verran pilots suggests that the first real gains came from a plainer source: not a sharper measurement of any single run, but the patient stacking of many ordinary ones, each of them mediocre, until the average at last held steady. What later looked like a triumph of the instrument was, for one crucial century, a triumph of the archive. The pilots learned the line not by measuring it better, but by remembering it together.
— Marta Løvold
