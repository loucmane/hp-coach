### Text

The Baltic keeps its shipwrecks the way few seas do. Its water is cold, nearly fresh, and low in oxygen, and the shipworm that reduces a sunken hull to lace within a decade in warmer seas cannot breed there at all. So the seabed off the Swedish and Estonian coasts still holds wooden ships that went down three and four centuries ago with their masts standing and their carvings crisp. When one such wreck — a merchantman the survey team labelled the Sabina, lying upright in ninety metres of water off Gotland — was mapped in detail two winters ago, the first argument was not about how it had sunk. It was about what to do with it: haul the timbers up into the light, or leave them where the dark had kept them safe.

Some answers came without disturbing the wreck at all. A dendrochronologist at the Kalvik maritime unit, Tobias Renlund, took thin cores from a dozen exposed frames and matched their ring patterns against a master chronology built from Baltic oak. The wood, he found, had been felled in a narrow window around the winter of 1627, and its ring signature pointed not to Gotland but to forests on the southern shore, hundreds of miles from where the ship had come to rest. That alone unsettled the local story that the Sabina had been built and sailed as a Gotland vessel. Renlund is careful about how far the dating reaches. The outer, datable sapwood survived on only a handful of the cored timbers, so the felling year holds firmly for the main hull planking; for the lighter timbers, which may have come from later repairs, he will say only that the evidence is "consistent, not settled".

The deeper question — raise or leave — the team chose to test rather than argue. Twenty loose frames that had already worked free of the wreck were divided in two. Half were brought to the surface and put through the long conservation bath that waterlogged oak needs, soaked for years in polyethylene glycol to replace the water in the cells before the wood is allowed to dry. The other half were reburied in the cold mud beside the wreck and left under instruments that logged their condition season after season. For the first few years the comparison seemed almost cruelly one-sided. The raised, treated frames were clean, catalogued, and on their way to a display case; the reburied ones were invisible, muddy, and doing nothing anyone could photograph. Read only that far, and leaving good timber in the dark looked like a failure of nerve.

Then the longer record came in, and it ran the other way. As the raised timbers dried, they began to do what the salvaged wood of older museum ships had done before them: sulphur compounds soaked up during centuries on the seabed reacted with oxygen and moisture in the gallery air, and a faint bloom of acid crept through the grain, splitting some frames along the ring lines and forcing conservators back for repeated, costly treatment. The reburied frames, sealed again in cold and airless mud, simply held. Season after season the instruments reported almost no change; the very conditions that had preserved the ship for four hundred years went on preserving it. What the museum had gained in access it was slowly paying for in decay, while the timber it had left behind sat unspoiled and unseen.

The unit's senior conservator, Marit Oldenburg, draws a hard line from all this. "Every plank we lift, we begin to lose," she told me. "The sea is the only conservator that never sends a bill. Leave them down there — always, every wreck, without exception — and let the water go on doing what no laboratory can." The record from the Sabina is more divided than her rule allows. It does not show that raising a wreck is a mistake; it shows that raising one is a bargain, in which the price is a slow chemical unmaking that the seabed spares you. A ship left in the mud is a ship no visitor will ever stand before and no student will ever measure by hand. Whether that trade is worth making depends on what the wreck is wanted for — and that is a question the instruments cannot answer. The dark keeps the wood. It keeps it from us, too.
— Elias Frisk
